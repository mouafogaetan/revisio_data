📄 Lesson 5: Applying for a Job

Terminale A (Literary Stream) · English · Module 1: The Individual and Society · Chapter 2: The World of Work

📌 Key concepts to remember

🔍 1. Analysing a Job Advertisement

A job advertisement contains essential information that helps you decide if you are a good candidate. You must identify:

  • Job title / Position – e.g., Sales Assistant, Office Clerk, Intern.
  • Requirements – qualifications, experience, skills (e.g., "Bachelor's degree", "fluent in English", "team player").
  • Salary & benefits – payment, working hours, holidays.
  • How to apply – send CV + cover letter, deadline, contact person.
💡 Exam tip: In reading comprehension, always underline the keywords (requirements, deadline, contact) before answering.

✍️ 2. Writing a Simple Cover Letter (formal letter)

A cover letter accompanies your CV and convinces the employer to meet you. For Terminale level, use this structure:

  • Your address & date (top right).
  • Employer's address (left, below your address).
  • Subject line: Application for [Job title].
  • Salutation: Dear Sir or Madam, (if name unknown) or Dear Mr./Ms. [Name].
  • Paragraph 1: Introduce yourself and state the job you are applying for.
  • Paragraph 2: Highlight your qualifications, skills and why you are suitable.
  • Paragraph 3: Mention your motivation, willingness to learn, and availability.
  • Closing: Yours faithfully, (if started with Dear Sir/Madam) or Yours sincerely, (if used a name) + signature.

⚙️ 3. Grammar: Modals of Advice & Obligation

In job application contexts, we use specific modals to express what is necessary or recommended.

  • Should / Shouldn't = advice (what is a good idea).
    Example: "You should include a professional email address on your CV."
  • Have to / Has to = external obligation (rules, requirements).
    Example: "Applicants have to submit their documents before Friday."
  • Must / Mustn't = strong obligation / prohibition (often official).
    Example: "You must attach your certificates; you mustn't forget the deadline."
📚 Remember: "should" is softer advice, "have to" expresses external rules, "must" is personal or official strong obligation.

💡 Application examples & model tasks

📢 Example 1: Reading a job advertisement

📌 Exam-style exercise
JOB ADVERTISEMENT:
Position: Junior Administrative Assistant
Location: Douala, Cameroon
Requirements: A-level certificate (Baccalauréat) + good command of English and French. Proficient in MS Office.
Duties: Answer phone calls, manage schedules, assist the manager.
Salary: competitive, based on experience.
To apply: Send CV and cover letter to recruit@company.cm by 30th November 2025.

✅ Task (Reading comprehension): What are the THREE main requirements for this job?
Answer: 1) A-level certificate (Baccalauréat). 2) Good command of English and French. 3) Proficiency in MS Office.

💬 Tip: In the exam, always read the job ad twice and highlight the "requirements" or "qualifications" section.

✉️ Example 2: Writing a cover letter (from a student to a company)

Context: You are a Terminale A student. You saw an advertisement for a part-time library assistant at “City Library, Yaoundé”. Requirements: love for books, good communication, ability to work on Saturdays. Write a short cover letter (approx. 120 words).
📝 MODEL ANSWER:
Your address: 12 Rue des Écoles, Yaoundé
Date: 14th April 2026
Employer: City Library, PO Box 453, Yaoundé

Subject: Application for Library Assistant position

Dear Sir or Madam,

I am writing to apply for the Library Assistant position advertised on your notice board on 10th April. I am a Terminale A student at Lycée Général Leclerc, and I have always been passionate about reading and helping others.
I possess good communication skills in both English and French. I am organised, punctual, and I love working in a quiet environment. I believe I am suitable for this role because I have volunteered in my school’s library for two years, where I learned to classify books and assist students.
I am available every Saturday and during school holidays. I look forward to the possibility of working with your team.

Yours faithfully,
Your Name

🔍 Why this letter works: It includes the job reference, relevant skills, personal motivation, and polite closing. The grammar uses present perfect (“I have volunteered”) and present simple.

🧠 Remember: always adapt the letter to the specific job. Do not copy without changing the job title and skills.

📘 Example 3: Using modals (should / have to / must) in job applications

Complete these sentences from a job search guide with the correct modal: should / shouldn't / have to / must.
  1. You _______ proofread your cover letter twice; spelling mistakes look unprofessional.
  2. Applicants _______ send their diplomas along with the CV. It’s a strict rule.
  3. You _______ lie about your experience; honesty is essential.
  4. She _______ complete an online test before the interview – it’s part of the recruitment process.

✅ Answers & explanation:
1. should – advice: it’s highly recommended.
2. must / have to (both possible: “must” for strong obligation, “have to” for external rule).
3. shouldn’t – negative advice / what is wrong to do.
4. has to (singular) / have to – external obligation from the company.

💪 Exam tip: In writing tasks, using modals correctly shows a good command of nuance. Use "must" for official requirements, "should" for recommendations.

📚 Key vocabulary for job application tasks

🔤 Essential terms (English ⇔ Context)

  • Job advertisement – an announcement in a newspaper or online about a vacant position.
  • Cover letter / application letter – a formal letter explaining why you are the best candidate.
  • CV (curriculum vitae) – a document listing your education, work experience, and skills.
  • Qualifications – degrees, certificates or skills that make you suitable.
  • Experience – previous jobs or volunteering activities.
  • Deadline – the final date to submit your application.
  • References – people who can recommend you (e.g., teachers, former employers).
  • Applicant / candidate – a person who applies for a job.
  • Interview – a formal meeting to assess the candidate.
🌍 Use these words in your composition: “As an applicant, I must respect the deadline and submit my CV along with a convincing cover letter.”

⚠️ Common pitfalls / Avoid these mistakes

  • ❌ Mistake 1: Forgetting the structure of a formal letter (no addresses, no subject line).
    ✅ Correction: Always include your address, date, employer’s address, and a subject line.
  • ❌ Mistake 2: Using informal language like "Hi" or "Thanks a lot!" in a cover letter.
    ✅ Correction: Keep a polite and professional tone: "Dear Sir or Madam", "Yours faithfully".
  • ❌ Mistake 3: Confusing "should" and "have to". "You should apply" = advice; "You have to apply before Friday" = obligation.
    ✅ Tip: In exam writing tasks, read the context: is it a rule or a recommendation?
  • ❌ Mistake 4: Repeating the same vocabulary (e.g., "good" too many times).
    ✅ Use synonyms: skilled, qualified, motivated, experienced, reliable.
  • ❌ Mistake 5: Not mentioning the job title or the source of the advertisement.
    ✅ Always write: "I am applying for the position of [Job title] as advertised in [newspaper/website]."

📝 Task: Write a short cover letter

Job ad: “We are looking for a volunteer English tutor for primary school children. Requirements: love for teaching, patience, and at least an intermediate level of English. Saturdays from 9am to 12pm. Send your application to education@ngo.org by 10 May.”

Your turn: Write a 100–120 word cover letter. Use the structure: introduction → skills & motivation → availability → polite closing. Include at least one modal (should / have to / must) and 3 vocabulary words from the list.

📖 After writing, check: Did you include your address, date, subject line, salutation, and signature? Is the tone polite?
