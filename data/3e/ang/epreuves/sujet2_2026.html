Part Two: Reading Comprehension (20 marks)

A Civic Lesson

In a small village in the Centre Region of Cameroon, the elders organized a meeting to discuss the upcoming presidential election. Unlike previous years, the youth were reluctant to participate. They felt their voices did not matter. Tata Albert, a retired teacher and respected elder, decided to address the issue.

"My children," he began, "democracy is like a bicycle. If you do not pedal forward (which means to vote and express your views), you will fall. Our nation's motto is 'Peace, Work, Fatherland.' But peace without justice is not possible, and justice requires your participation."

He reminded them of the history of their fight for independence. "Our ancestors fought so that we could have the right to choose. If you stay at home on election day, you are giving away your power to those who might not have your best interests at heart."

The youth listened carefully. Marie, a 16-year-old student, raised her hand. "But Tata, we are not allowed to vote yet. What can we do?" Tata Albert smiled. "You can educate your families, you can promote peace on social media, and you can learn about the candidates. Citizenship starts before you turn 20. It is a daily habit of love for your community."

That day, the youth left the meeting with a new sense of responsibility. They understood that living together (le vivre-ensemble) requires effort from everyone, young and old.

QUESTIONS (10 marks)
